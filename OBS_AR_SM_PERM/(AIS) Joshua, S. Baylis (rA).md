## **Message of the Book**

**Instead of pridefully grasping at God’s holiness, Israel should walk humbly with strength and courage according to the Word of the LORD—God’s true Image—symbolized by the witness of the altar and defined by the dynamic of the Ban, which is God’s self-giving love through the coming Seed.**

---

## **Definitions**

### **The Word of the LORD**

The Word is the true _Image of God_ in the absence of Adamic image-bearers, who, having gained the knowledge of good and evil, can now only reflect themselves. The Word becomes the new Image, carrying forward the hope of Abel, the firstborn, and the sacrificial dynamic of Genesis 3:15. It is the revelation of God’s character, pointing to a Seed who would crush the serpent not by human might, but through substitutionary death.

### **The Ban (חרם)**

The ban refers to God’s claim over what is holy. It is not merely destruction—it is God’s prerogative to judge sin and preserve holiness. It enacts the Genesis 3:15 dynamic: the Seed and the Serpent die together. In Passover, the lamb and Egypt are judged. In Jericho, all is devoted to destruction except the one marked by faith (Rahab). To take what is banned is to claim God’s holiness as one’s own.

### **Holiness in the Midst**

God’s holiness is not a detached moral category; it is His love expressed through judgment and substitution—embodied in the image of the bronze serpent lifted up (Num. 21). In being bitten and lifted up, God shows Himself uniquely holy: able to absorb judgment for the sake of others.

### **Binary Development**

God’s righteous ones always look beyond themselves: Eve and her Seed, Lamech and his son, Abraham and Isaac. This pattern—trusting in another—is opposed to self-righteousness. Israel is to embody this by walking behind the ark, not ahead of it.

---

## **Relation to the Biblical Narrative**

Joshua continues the momentum of Numbers and Deuteronomy by showing how the holiness of God is to be honored in the land. The ark, Passover, and the ban all reinforce the central theological point: _God’s holiness is revealed in His self-giving Word, not in Israel’s strength._ Every major event in Joshua plays on the tension between humility and pride, between honoring the Word and presuming upon God.

## **Literary Structure**

## **I. (Ch. 1–6) Setting**

God fights for Israel when they walk by faith—not by taking holiness for themselves. Rahab, a Gentile harlot, is saved through Israel’s disobedient vow, showing that salvation depends on God’s holiness, not human merit.

The ark of the covenant circling Jericho forms a microcosm of how Israel should think. The ark, containing the law outside of Israel’s heart, symbolized that righteousness was not inherently theirs. Victory came not because of Israel’s righteousness, but because of the wickedness of the nations and God’s covenant faithfulness (Deut. 9).

Joshua’s encounter with the captain of the LORD’s host reinforces this truth. When Joshua asks, “Are you for us or for our adversaries?” the answer is “No” (Josh. 5:13–14). Israel is not the protagonist. God alone is.

Rahab, remarkably, mirrors the ark—both are vessels of mercy amid judgment. The scarlet cord links Rahab to Passover and to the scarlet thread of Zerah, creating a deliberate literary parallel.[^2]

**Key Point:** Rahab mirrors the ark—both are vessels of God’s mercy amid judgment.

---

## **II. (Ch. 7–9) Conflict → Tension**

Israel cannot help but grasp at God’s holiness. Achan, a descendant of Zerah (Judah’s line), is destroyed for stealing the banned items.[^3] Joshua, the leader, also errs by trusting his own judgment and making a covenant with the Gibeonites.

Even the bearers of the Seed and the Mosaic leader fail. Both attempt to take for themselves what belongs to the LORD alone.

This drives the literary tension toward the need for a Greater Moses—one who will not fail and who will Himself become the true holiness of God under the ban.

**Key Point:** Even the bearers of the Seed and the leader of Israel fall short—only a Greater Moses can fulfill God’s holy mission.

### **A. Achan and Rahab**

There is an inverse relationship between Rahab and Achan. Rahab, the Gentile harlot, is saved in the midst of destruction by the holiness of God, while Achan, from the tribe of Zerah, is destroyed after the destruction because of self-righteousness.

This emphasizes that holiness is not located in Israel itself, but in the Seed within Israel. Even the most unclean may be saved by that core.

The literary clue includes the repeated phrase “all that was to him/her,” signaling a deliberate parallel between Rahab and Achan (cf. Josh. 6:23; 7:24).[^4]

### **B. Joshua and the Gibeonites**

Joshua acts foolishly by leaning on his own understanding rather than inquiring of the LORD. In doing so, he violates Deuteronomy 7 by making covenant with those devoted to destruction.

The fullness of the tension is reached here: even the leader in the place of Moses fails through self-righteous judgment.

---

## **III. (Ch. 10–21) Rising Action**

Despite Israel’s failures, God still fights for them. He listens to Joshua’s flawed voice and halts the sun. The mercy shown to the Gibeonites echoes Rahab, showing that God honors faith even through human error.

The stopping of the sun and moon demonstrates God’s continued desire to rule creation through human images. Joshua’s flawed voice still moves heaven.

Caleb becomes the prototype of faithful Israel. While others hesitate, Caleb trusts God and receives Hebron.

The inheritance is distributed not because of Israel’s righteousness (Deut. 9:4), but because of God’s faithfulness.

**Key Point:** The inheritance is distributed under the shadow of grace, not because of Israel’s righteousness, but because of God’s faithfulness.

---

## **IV. (Ch. 22:1–29) Climax**

The altar of witness becomes the narrative’s climax. It nearly sparks civil war but ultimately reveals that Israel understands the need for a shared witness to the altar—the place where God’s holiness is acknowledged.

The trans-Jordan tribes build an altar, and Israel initially assumes rebellion equivalent to Achan’s sin—taking holiness for oneself.

Instead, the altar proves to be a witness that their identity is rooted in the true altar of the LORD.

**Key Point:** The altar is a sign: God’s holiness belongs to Him alone. It unites Israel only when they humbly acknowledge that.

---

## **V. (Ch. 22:30–34) Falling Action**

Phineas blesses the trans-Jordan tribes, affirming their loyalty to the true altar. The altar of witness becomes a lasting testimony that Israel’s unity is rooted in the holiness of God, not national pride.

Phineas, who had previously defended God’s holiness at Baal-Peor, now confirms that the altar is not rebellion but humility.

The altar becomes a witness that “the LORD is in our midst.”

**Key Point:** Israel’s peace is rooted in shared humility before the holiness of God.

---

## **VI. (Ch. 23–24:28) Resolution**

Joshua tells the people they _will not_ be able to serve the LORD—He is too holy. Still, they vow to serve Him.

A stone is set up as witness, echoing Sinai and exposing the persistence of self-righteousness. Israel still believes they can perform the law.

Joshua’s statement is not pessimism but theological realism: they do not possess the character necessary to serve a holy God.

The Word of the LORD—not their effort—must remain central.

**Key Point:** Israel’s pride blinds them. Their own words will later testify against them.

### **A. Two Mountains**

Joshua reminds them of blessing and curse—Ebal and Gerizim. They must cling to the LORD, not to the nations.

The center of lawkeeping is not moral effort, but love for the LORD Himself.

### **B. God’s Speech**

God recounts Israel’s history and repeatedly says: “I gave,” “I sent,” “I brought.”

The point is unmistakable: it has always been God.

### **C. The Witness Stone**

Joshua sets up a stone that has “heard” Israel’s testimony. It becomes witness against them.

Like Sinai, Israel voluntarily enters covenant, but the narrative makes clear that this generation is no more capable than the last.

---

## **VII. (Ch. 24:29–33) Denouement**

Joshua dies. The bones of Joseph are buried.

Like Genesis, the story ends in death and hope. Israel possesses the land, but death remains. The promise is not yet fulfilled.

The burial of Joseph’s bones ties Joshua back to Genesis and reminds the reader that the land itself was never the final goal.

The narrative still waits for the coming Seed who will truly fulfill Genesis 3:15.

**Key Point:** Joshua reaches the land, but not the final rest.

---

## **Summary**

Joshua is a narrative of contrasts:

- Between the ark and self-righteous striving
- Between Rahab’s salvation and Achan’s destruction
- Between the true altar and false attempts to claim God’s holiness

In every contrast, the message is clear:

_The Word of the LORD—the coming Image, the sacrificial Seed—is Israel’s only hope._

To walk with strength and courage is to walk humbly behind that Word, trusting in the holiness of God revealed through the ban, through Passover, through the altar, and ultimately, through Jesus—the Holy One who was Himself devoted to destruction for the sake of the world.

## **LIRF Placement**

**Location in LIRF:** `OBS_AR_SM_5/5_PERM_12/15`  
**Narrative Role:** _Stabilization from Movement within the Observable Realm_  
**Philosophical Axis:** _High Permanence (12/15)_

The book of Joshua sits at a pivotal point in the LIRF grid: it represents a moment of stabilizing hope after Israel’s wilderness movement, yet the permanence achieved is partial and anticipatory.

As a historical narrative, it concretely depicts Israel entering and possessing the land—the observable manifestation of the covenant promise. However, the stabilization is marked by deep fragility: the holiness of God is dangerously misunderstood, and the hearts of the people remain unchanged.

At this grid point, the narrative calls for strong and courageous obedience, not grounded in nationalistic zeal or self-righteousness, but in reverence for the Word of the LORD, which functions as the true Image of God in their midst.

The placement at PERM 12/15 reflects the nearly complete structure God establishes through conquest, covenant, and cultic order—but this structure is held together only by God’s ongoing grace.

The ban (ḥērem), understood here as the expression of God’s holy love that destroys evil and redeems through substitutionary sacrifice, becomes the interpretive key.

Israel’s failure lies in grasping for this holiness, rather than walking humbly under its witness.

Joshua thus models a temporary stabilization—a prototype of the eschatological order—pointing forward to the Greater Joshua, in whom God’s holiness and love will be fully revealed through death and resurrection.
